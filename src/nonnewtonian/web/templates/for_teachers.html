For teachers

If you just need a slide

You don't need an account. Open a textbook, go to the chapter you're teaching, and read the scientists placed there. Each one has a "Download slide" link if you want one for your deck.

Running the assignment with your own class

Per-class collections, where your students research and place scientists in your textbook and you moderate what goes public, aren't ready yet. In the meantime, here is the assignment I use. Adapt it however you like:

Assignment (research a new scientist)

This course and textbook have largely focused on ideas developed by white, European men. Your task is to broaden that focus: write a paragraph about someone who has made a significant contribution to physics and who is not a white European man (contemporary or historical), someone you don't already know about or think should be better known. Say who they are, what they contributed, why it matters, and something about their life and how it shaped their work. Then, using your textbook's table of contents, tell us which chapter their contribution belongs in. Include a link to a photo if you can, and cite your sources (Wikipedia is fine, just don't plagiarize). If you'd like your name credited publicly, include it; if not, leave it off.

Assignment (adopt & re-place an existing entry)

Choose a scientist already in the collection, verify their writeup and check it for plagiarism, add at least one new fact or image, and place them in our textbook, telling us which chapter and why.

A note for K–12 classes: student work is published on a public page. When class collections launch, the default will credit students by first name and last initial only, and you'll be able to require full anonymity. You remain responsible for your school's media-release and parental-consent policies.
