About this project

Every year, my intro students work through a standard textbook and meet a long line of physicists who, to within rounding error, all look like Newton. I don't think that's an accident, and I don't think it's harmless. Students come away with a quiet sense of who does physics and who doesn't.

This started as a class assignment: research someone who made a significant contribution to physics and who isn't a white European man, write a paragraph about them, and figure out where in the textbook their work belongs. The writeups became a shared, chapter-indexed resource, so a teacher covering, say, energy or nuclear physics can pull up a relevant scientist and a slide to go with them.

Why it works

There is evidence this does more than make me feel better about the syllabus. Giving students repeated, structured exposure to scientists who break the stereotype measurably shifts their sense of who does science (Schinske et al., CBE—Life Sciences Education, 2016).
